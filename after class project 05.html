The 9 Classical Dances of India are:

Indian classical dances, rooted in ancient traditions, trace their origins
to the Natya Shastra (c. 2nd century BCE–2nd century CE), a foundational
treatise on performance arts. Originally performed in temples as devotional
offerings to deities, these dances blended spiritual stories with precise
gestures (mudras), expressions (abhinaya), and structured techniques.
Developed in regional courts and temples, these art forms, such as
Bharatanatyam, Kathak, and Kathakali, evolved into distinct styles
(usually eight recognized by the Sangeet Natak Akademi) that were
later revived and codified in the 20th century, merging and regional
folk elements with classical principles.

    1.Kuchipudi (Andhra Pradesh)
    History: Developed in the 17th century in Andhra Pradesh, combining dance, drama, and song.
    Style: Known for fluid movements, faster tempo, and often performed on a brass plate.
    2.Bharatanatyam (Tamil Nadu)
    History: Considered the oldest, it was traditionally performed in Tamil Nadu temples by devadasis
    (priestesses). It was known as Sadhir Attam before its revival.
    Style: Characterized by fixed upper torsos, bent knees, and expressive hand gestures(mudras).
    3.Kathak (Uttar Pradesh)
    History: Originated in North India from Kathakars (storytellers). It evolved from temple
    stories to entertainment in Persian-influenced royal courts.
    Style: Known for fast footwork, intricate spins, and storytelling.
    4.Kathakali (Kerala)
    History: A 17th-century drama-dance form from Kerala, drawing from Krishnattam and Ramanattam.
    Style: Features elaborate makeup and costumes to represent characters like gods and demons.
    5.Odissi (Odisha)
    History: Originating in Odisha, this temple dance was historically performed by Maharis (temple dancers).
    Style: Known for Tribhangi (three-bend posture) and fluid torso movements, focusing on themes from Gitagovinda.
    6.Manipuri (Manipur)
    History: Originated in Manipur and gained popularity in the 18th century, particularly through Rasleela (dances of Radha-Krishna).
    Style: Gentle, soft, and devotional, often performed with cymbals and the Pung drum.
    7.Mohiniyattam (Kerala)
    History: Known as the dance of the enchantress, it is a feminine, graceful dance from Kerala with influences from Bharatanatyam and Kathakali.
    Style: Focuses on slow, swaying movements and gentle gestures.
    8.Sattriya (Assam)
    History: Introduced in the 15th century by the Bhakti saint Sankaradeva in Assam, originally performed in Satras (monasteries).
    Style: A vibrant mix of dance, drama, and devotional stories.
